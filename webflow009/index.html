  THE NATURAL WONDERS OF

Avon Gorge &
Leigh Woods

Did you know that Bristol is home to a wide variety of wildlife, including many rare and significant species? Avon Gorge is a Special Area of Conservation and a designated Site of Special Scientific Interest, and across the River Avon, parts of Leigh Woods are also a National Nature Reserve.

The dramatic landscape of Avon Gorge creates it’s own microclimate, slightly warmer than its surroundings. This has enabled endemic plant species to evolve here, such as Bristol Rock Cress, Bristol Onion and the Bristol Whitebeam.

The area is also home to a variety of birds. Bullfinches, woodpeckers and jays all inhabit the area. The cliffs of the gorge are a breeding site for peregrine falcons - if you’re lucky you might spot them diving as they hunt for prey.

Across the river Avon lies Leigh Woods - home to a rich and diverse range of insects such as moths, butterflies and beetles, some of which are very rare. One such example is the silky wave moth, which can only be found on two other sites in the UK.

Seven species of bat live in Leigh Woods, including the rare greater and lesser horseshoe bats and Daubenton’s bat. The bats make use of the old trees and caves for roosting.

The ancient woodland is home to many veteran trees such as yew, oak and beech. Large mammals such as roe deer and foxes can be spotted… if you’re patient and lucky. Lots of different mice and vowel species also live in the woods.
